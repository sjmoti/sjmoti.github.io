Before we dive in this week, we want to highlight something you might have missed: Mayor Liccardo’s recent public disagreements with FCC Commissioner Brendan Carr.

First of all, what is all this about?

Mayor Liccardo’s New York Times op-ed, detailed how the FCC’s recent policies force “a taxpayer subsidy to Big Telecom companies”, threatening landmark deals San José signed earlier this year with telecommunications companies. These deals were praised by AT&T, government officials, and non-partisan institutions as productive and collaborative deals. These would streamline the deployment of internet-providing broadband small cells while also funding a Digital Inclusion Fund that will be used to help close the digital divide in San José, with initiatives like providing internet access for students in East Side Union High School District.

Threatening initiatives that provide internet for students in order to subsidize Big Telecom's profits?

Yes, perhaps unsurprisingly, FCC Commissioner Brendan Carr, a Republican appointee to the FCC under the current President, who voted alongside FCC Chairman Pai to repeal net neutrality and who previously represented Big Telecom clients, did not sit well with Mayor Liccardo’s op-ed. He mounted attacks on Twitter as well as with an op-ed in the Mercury News to condemn our “record of broadband failure”. You can read Mayor Liccardo’s response op-ed, where he scrutinizes the falsehoods in Carr’s op-ed: Opinion: FCC proves it doesn’t get the innovation economy.

Photo: Brendan Carr and Mayor Liccardo discuss the New York Times op-ed on Twitter

Why you should care:

Since these issue areas are often technical and complex, it is easy for them to fly under the radar of most media outlets. Like Mayor Liccardo mentioned back in April: “It’s often lost on the public just how badly they’re being screwed.” On top of that, unlike other areas of government where the President appointed loyalists or business people with little to no experience/government experiencein their subject area (read: Dr. Carson as Housing Secretary, Rick Perry as Energy Secretary, Betsy DeVos as Education Secretary, etc), this administration’s appointees to the FCC are well-versed and knowledgeable on the subject area of communication technologies. With relatively low media attention, they have often been able to subsidize Big Telecommunication companies at the expense of taxpayers, often spreading misleading information about their underlying motivations.

As Mayor Liccardo mentions in his op-ed, these falsehoods “don’t deserve credence, but they do deserve scrutiny”. Nearly two dozen cities, with support from the National League of Cities & the US Conference of Mayors have joined San José to sue to FCC to restore the rights of local communities to negotiate our own deals. And “if the FCC cannot craft policies that balance the public interest with technological progress, we will.” You can also read Shireen'ssop-edson the issue published early this week. We hope that this brief summary will help you understand and communicate with residents about this issue.

Innovation Project Spotlight

This week, given recent focus on our small cell deployments, we wanted to highlight the ramping up of our small cell deployment. Earlier this year, the City Council unanimously approved deals with three of the largest telecom firms to deploy 4,000+ small cells across the city. This small cell network would see a 10-100x improvement in download speeds for residents, while remaining proceeds would go towards work to close the digital divide in San José. You can read more about these deals, including reading relevant agreements on our website here: Small Cell Networks.

Photo: Small Cells being connected to fiber, and later fully deployed in San José City Hall Plaza

To date, we have approved 92 small cells to be deployed, with 4000+ more to come given last month’s agreements. These cells have been primarily deployed in the Downtown area due to construction timelines. However, the rest of the 4000+ will be distributed through the City. We will also be collaborating on an annual basis to ensure that a minimum of 10% are located in underserved areas. Across 3 mobile carriers, over 500 street light locations are already reserved. In the first quarter of 2019, the City expects to reach averages of 150 permits per month across the three mobile carriers, delivering permits within 30 days. For all of 2019, we expect over 1,000 small cells to be permitted.

Over the next 3-4 years, the City will continue to collaborate with the carriers to not only provide streamlined permitting but also identify underserved areas that need improved service for economic development, digital inclusion, and preparation for Smart City applications as identified in the 2017 Broadband and Digital Inclusion Strategy. Many thanks to J. Guevara, Broadband Manager for the City Manager’s Office and the rest of his team and collaborators for their work in ramping up the deployment of these small cells!

MOTI's Top Reads of the Week

A map reflecting Twitter activity surrounding fake and conspiracy news stories among the most followed accounts around the 2016 presidential election. Courtesy Knight Foundation.

  • From Knight Foundation: Why Combatting Fake News Requires People and Technology--Working Together
    • Concerns about fake news, have seemed to reach a fever pitch, only growing in concern since the 2016 election. Earlier this year, Alex Jones from Infowars was (in)famously “deplatformed” on account of misinformation and hate speech violations. This month, the New York Times’ bombshell report on Facebook’s contract with a PR firm detailed how the firm might have engaged in misinformation tactics to clean up Facebook’s image about its mishandling of misinformation tactics (We know, it’s a little confusing. You can read Facebook’s response here: New York Times Update).With growing concerns around fake news, we were glad to return to this piece from October and the accompanying research report, which outline a path forward towards handling fake news.

    • “A few findings [from the Knight commissioned report] stood out.
      “First, the report found that the points of origin for misinformation were highly concentrated. Just       10 large sites accounted for 60 percent of the news links to misinformation...
      “Second. . . the researchers estimate that anywhere from a third to two-thirds of Twitter accounts spreading misinformation are automated accounts or ‘bots’...
      “If you’re a       glass half full person, these findings suggest a few bright spots. First, misinformation isn’t “everywhere,” even if it’s prevalent. Rather, it’s originating at known points that can be identified and potentially targeted...[emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: Amidst growing and valid concerns about the spread of fake news, we were glad to read this short, balanced piece about how we can fight to stop it. We also appreciate the acknowledgement of just how difficult this challenge is and recognizing how crucial it is to a healthy democracy.

Photo: Flickr/TriMet TriMet, the transit agency serving Portland, Ore. is exploring a next-generation transit signal prioritization system, among other technology improvements to reduce traffic congestion

  • From Gov Tech: How Transit Is Turning to Tech to Cut Through Congestion
    • This piece provides a look into the innovation of TriMet, which manages public transit in Portland, Ore. The systems, also being tested in rail operations, will hinge on data-heavy intelligent technology, so that more buses and trains can transport more people. The article also explores other innovative transportation initiatives like multimodal trip-planning and common payment systems.

    • “All of these approaches are the steps needed to solve congestion by making transit the more favorable option among city and suburban dwellers. “Philosophically, I think TriMet and the city of Portland, and the region here is still very committed to trying to promote and prioritize high-occupancy vehicles on frequent service routes, whether that’s light-rail vehicles or that’s buses,” said O’Connor.” [emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: We really appreciated the emphasis on leveraging technology to promote and prioritize high-occupancy vehicles on frequent service routes. As Mayor Liccardo often mentions, we are always looking to learn from other cities’ innovative ideas, as well as sharing our own ideas with our peers. We thought that the transportation initiatives TriMet in Portland is exploring are forward thinking and sustainable.

Photo via Route Fifty. The outside of a detention facility

  • From Route Fifty: Campaign to Automatically Expunge Criminal Convictions Targets States
    • This piece details a bipartisan initiative announced by the Center for American Progress that would automate criminal record-clearing in states for people who avoid committing another offense for a set amount of time. This could also allow states with unfilled labor positions access to an overlooked talent pool currently locked out of applying to many jobs.

    • “Most people with criminal records are unaware of their ability to clear them or unable to hire a lawyer to advocate for such action on their behalf, said Neera Tanden, president and CEO of CAP.
      “You move on, but society is not really ready for you to move on,” said Ronald Lewis of his two 2004 misdemeanor convictions that “haunted” him until Pennsylvania passed its clean slate law.” [emphasis added]

    • Why we liked this piece: In an era of such extreme political polarization, it’s a nice surprise to see an issue at the state level where politicians can work together. We also appreciated this issue as it shows how sometimes a relatively simple technological implementation can have a large impact on people's lives when there is a large volume of cases overwhelming the current systems.
