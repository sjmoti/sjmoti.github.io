Our Work Housing Code Enforcement

Prioritizing housing inspections with big data

Working with U. Chicago on predictive algorithms to prioritize housing inspections to keep our city safer
HOUSING DATA

What is the problem we are solving?

We want to make sure all our residents are living in safe conditions in San José. Apartment buildings with multiple dwelling units are inspected by the San José Code Enforcement team. However, we don’t have enough inspectors to visit every unit every year, so we have to prioritize which units we inspect first.

What did we do?

San José’s Code Enforcement Office conducts inspections for violations in “multiple housing” units – apartment buildings, fraternity and sorority houses, and hotels.The city had already implemented a tiered approach to inspect high risk properties more frequently. Introduced in 2015, it helped the city to identify serious violations in 59% of inspected properties, up from 46% in 2011-2014. However, we know that we could be even better at catching these violations – and therefore keeping the city safer – if we intelligently used the data we already have to prioritize inspections.


With the support of Bloomberg Philanthropies, we engaged University of Chicago’s Data Science for Social Good team to improve how we inspect these multiple housing units.

University of Chicago’s team spent a few days in San José, meeting with code enforcement staff to get advice and input on building the algorithms and go on ride-alongs with inspectors. The team then created more than 100,000 predictive models and back-tested these models against historical data: had we used each one of these predictive models at different points in the past, how good would we have been at catching major violations? Based on this evaluation, the team arrived at one model to conduct field testing.

The Code Enforcement team then spent several months testing the model against a control (our existing tiered approach). The result is an 8% lift in the rate of catching serious violations.


Throughout the process, the University of Chicago team worked closely with city staff, and San José is now working on transitioning the predictive model in-house. Head over to UChicago’s Data Science for Social Good blog for a detailed write-up of the project: Data-Driven Inspections for Safer Housing in San José, California.

Thanks to UChicago’s Data Science for Social Good team, the Code Enforcement team, and the City Manager's Office of Civic Innovation for making this project a success.
